What is Governance?

Governance defines the structure within which AI systems are designed, deployed, and monitored.

  • Mechanisms ‚Äî roles, responsibilities, and decision-making structures
  • Rules & Processes ‚Äî documented procedures and workflows
  • Policy Guidelines ‚Äî written directives governing behaviour
  • Guardrails ‚Äî limitations that prevent harmful or unintended actions
Definition Governance is the full set of mechanisms, rules, and limitations ‚Äî including policy guidelines and guardrails ‚Äî that shape how AI systems behave and are controlled.
Governance ¬∑ 2

Why Governance Matters

AI systems introduce unique risks that require structured oversight:

  • Toxic training data ‚Äî data may contain harmful, illegal, or biased content
  • Opacity ‚Äî models are complex and difficult to audit or explain
  • Adversarial attacks ‚Äî systems can be manipulated by malicious actors
  • Unforeseen interactions ‚Äî users engage with systems in unpredictable ways
  • Inaccuracy & bias ‚Äî outputs may be wrong or discriminatory
  • Significant impact ‚Äî unexpected failures can harm individuals and organizations

Why Regulation?

Regulation supports ‚Äî not hinders ‚Äî innovation by creating the rules of the game.

Innovation Benefits

  • Creates deliberate, thoughtful design processes
  • Constraints improve engineering (e.g. seatbelts, drug side-effects)
  • Prevents monopolies; creates SME opportunities

Clarity Benefits

  • Draws clear boundaries of acceptable behaviour
  • Enables informed decisions by businesses & consumers
  • Provides stability that attracts investment
Key Point If it is illegal to discriminate or deceive, it is illegal to discriminate or deceive using generative AI. Existing laws already apply.

Risks ¬∑ 1

Gen AI vs Narrow AI

Narrow AI

  • Purpose-built for specific tasks
  • Outputs primarily determined by data scientists' design decisions
  • Limited deployment contexts
  • Predictable, testable behaviour

Generative AI

  • Deployed across virtually every domain
  • Outputs largely determined by end-user prompts
  • Infinite contextual variation
  • Risk shifts from developers to users
Key Insight Change the prompt and you change the output. This fundamental shift makes end-user training and governance essential ‚Äî not optional.
Risks ¬∑ 2

Risk 1 ‚Äî Deployment Context Explosion

  • LLMs are deployed across every function: writing, coding, HR, finance, legal, customer support, and more
  • Use cases multiply across every company, department, role, and task
  • Impossible to pre-test model performance in every conceivable context of use
  • Risk mitigations designed for known use cases fail to cover emergent, unforeseen ones
Why It Matters Testing for "how will the model perform in the intended context of use" becomes phenomenally difficult to determine. The number of potential contexts is effectively limitless.
Risks ¬∑ 3

Risk 2 ‚Äî Monitoring Challenges at Scale

  • Developers cannot foresee all contexts, let alone mitigate every associated risk
  • AI can behave unexpectedly in novel contexts before anyone notices
  • Lack of monitoring means problems can compound silently over time
  • Requires ongoing surveillance of model behaviour "in the wild"
Best Practice Monitoring AI as it behaves in unpredictable contexts ‚Äî before things go off the rails with no one noticing ‚Äî is crucial and must be built into governance frameworks.
Risks ¬∑ 4

Risk 3 ‚Äî Hallucinations

LLMs routinely generate false, fabricated, or misleading information ‚Äî known as hallucinations.

  • Gen AI produces plausible-sounding but factually incorrect outputs
  • Without training, users may not fact-check AI-generated content
  • Organizations risk internal and external documents containing false information
  • Human oversight remains essential but requires enhanced training vs. narrow AI
Example Risk Employees regularly create client-facing reports with false data because they trusted LLM outputs without verification ‚Äî damaging credibility and potentially creating legal liability.
Risks ¬∑ 5

Risk 4 ‚Äî Responsible Prompting

Since outputs are driven by prompts, how users interact with LLMs determines both output quality and risk exposure.

  • Users may inadvertently include sensitive or confidential data in prompts
  • LLMs connected to third-party services may transmit company data externally
  • Prompt injection attacks can manipulate model behaviour maliciously
  • Organisations need prompt governance policies and user training programs
Rule of Thumb Never input company-sensitive data into an LLM that sends data to a third party without explicit authorization and a clear data processing agreement.
Risks ¬∑ 6

Risk 5 ‚Äî Multi-Point Risk Assessment

Most organisations procure pre-trained models from providers (Microsoft, Google, Anthropic, OpenAI) ‚Äî creating a layered risk landscape.

  • Provider-level mitigations are generic ‚Äî not tailored to specific enterprise contexts
  • Enterprise-level fine-tuning creates new, organisation-specific risks
  • Department-level customisation adds yet another layer requiring its own assessment
  • Risk assessments may be needed before and after every modification ‚Äî but this is not clearly defined
The Core Challenge Who should perform what risk assessment, at what point in the lifecycle, while balancing thoroughness against operational efficiency? This remains unresolved.
Risks ¬∑ 7

Risk 6 ‚Äî End-User Training Deficits

Gen AI tools require every employee to understand responsible use ‚Äî a new and often overlooked training need.

  • Narrow AI training focused mainly on data scientists and technical teams
  • Gen AI training must reach every user across every function
  • Topics must include: responsible prompting, data privacy, hallucination awareness, output verification
  • Insufficient training = organisational risk amplified at scale

Key Takeaways

  • Risk shifts to users ‚Äî responsible prompting is now a core organisational competency
  • Pre-emptive mitigation is insufficient ‚Äî the breadth of contexts requires continuous monitoring
  • Monitoring is a governance requirement ‚Äî not an optional add-on
  • Risk ownership must be defined ‚Äî multi-stakeholder AI chains create accountability gaps
  • Hallucinations are structural ‚Äî workflows must permanently account for them, not wait for a fix
  • Training must be universal ‚Äî every employee who touches gen AI tools needs responsible AI training
Bottom Line Generative AI requires a fundamentally different governance approach ‚Äî one built on continuous monitoring, distributed risk ownership, and organisation-wide digital literacy.
