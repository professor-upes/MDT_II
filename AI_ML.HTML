ANI vs AGI

Definitions

ANI ‚Äî Artificial Narrow Intelligence

  • Specialized systems for specific tasks
  • Dominates real-world business AI today
  • Examples: fraud scoring, churn prediction, route optimization

AGI ‚Äî Artificial General Intelligence

  • General-purpose intelligence across domains
  • Human-like ability to transfer learning broadly
  • Still hypothetical (not deployed in businesses)

Business focus: design many ANI systems aligned to specific KPIs; governance and workflow integration matter more than ‚Äúintelligence hype.‚Äù

Machine Learning (ML)

Definition

  • Machine Learning is a subset of AI that enables systems to learn patterns from historical data and make predictions or decisions without being explicitly programmed with rules for every scenario.

Core Shift

Traditional Programming

  • Rules + Data ‚Üí Output
  • Good when rules are stable and explicit
  • Breaks when patterns change or become complex

Machine Learning

  • Data + Outcomes ‚Üí Model ‚Üí Future Prediction
  • Learns patterns from history
  • Adapts better to complexity and scale

Managerial View

ML is a prediction engine that improves decisions at scale.
Value appears when predictions change actions inside a workflow.

Inventory Management Problem

Scenario: You Start a Grocery Business

  • You fill shelves with products. Inventory becomes hard to manage:
  • overstock ‚Üí waste
  • understock ‚Üí lost sales

The Real Decision

Decision: ‚ÄúHow much inventory should I stock for each item next week?‚Äù

Why ‚ÄúLast Month Sales‚Äù is Not Enough

  • Seasonality (festivals, weekends, weather)
  • Promotions and price changes
  • New store / new city scaling effects
  • Supply constraints and lead times

Scaling Explosion

  • One store + 200 items = manageable
  • 1000 stores + 20,000 SKUs = complexity explodes

ML helps by forecasting demand and recommending replenishment decisions‚Äîreducing waste (Cost) and stockouts (Revenue).

Artificial Intelligence (AI), Machine Learning (ML), Deep Learning

Types of ML

Three Learning Types

Supervised Learning

  • Learn from historical data with known outcomes
  • Typical problems: classification, regression
  • Examples: churn, credit risk, demand prediction

Unsupervised Learning

  • No outcome labels; find structure/patterns
  • Typical problems: clustering, anomaly detection
  • Examples: customer segments, unusual transactions

Reinforcement Learning (brief)

  • Learn by trial-and-error via rewards
  • Best for sequential decisions
  • Examples: dynamic pricing, ad bidding, routing policies

Problem Types (What you predict)

  • Classification: yes/no (fraud?)
  • Regression: number (demand?)
  • Clustering: groups (segments)
  • Anomaly: unusual (suspicious)

Teaching anchor: ML is not ‚Äúone thing.‚Äù Start by naming the problem type, then choose an algorithm.

Deep Learning (DL) & Data Science (DS)

Deep Learning (DL)

  • Definition: Deep Learning is a subset of ML that uses patterns in large datasets.

When DL is used

Images Speech Video

Examples

  • Aadhaar face authentication
  • Medical imaging startups (e.g., image-based screening/diagnostics)

DL is most useful when the input is unstructured (image/audio/text) and scale + complexity are high.

Data Science (DS)

  • Definition: Data Science is the broader discipline that combines statistics and business insight to extract value from data.

What DS includes

  • Data cleaning (quality, missing values, standardization)
  • Exploratory analysis (patterns, trends, segmentation insights)
  • Statistical modeling (inference, experimentation)
  • ML modeling (prediction + classification)
  • Business communication (insight-to-action storytelling)

MBA takeaway: DS is the end-to-end business discipline; ML/DL are tools inside it. Value comes from decisions + KPI impact, not models alone.

ML Algorithms : Linear Regression

What business problem it solves

  • Predicts a number: sales, demand, cost, delivery time, spend

Intuition (no math)

  • Finds the ‚Äúbest-fit relationship‚Äù between drivers (price, ads, season) and an outcome (sales).
  • Great baseline when you want to explain drivers to leadership.

Use when

  • Need interpretability
  • Relationships are roughly linear
  • Baseline model for planning

Avoid when

  • Highly complex interactions dominate
  • Data patterns are strongly non-linear

Applications + KPI impact

  • Demand planning (inventory): Cost ‚Üì waste, Revenue ‚Üë fewer stockouts
  • Marketing mix baseline: Revenue ‚Üë, Cost ‚Üì wasted spend

ML Algorithms : Logistic Regression

What business problem it solves

  • Predicts probability of an event: churn, default, fraud, conversion

Intuition (no math)

  • Turns customer/transaction data into a risk score (0‚Äì1).
  • Business chooses a threshold: ‚ÄúIf risk > 0.7, intervene.‚Äù

Use when

  • Need explainable baseline
  • Risk scoring and policy decisions
  • Stakeholders want clarity

Avoid when

  • Patterns are highly non-linear
  • You need maximum predictive accuracy

üìå Applications + KPI impact

  • Credit risk scoring: Risk ‚Üì (defaults), Revenue ‚Üë (better approvals)
  • Churn targeting: Revenue ‚Üë (retention), Cost ‚Üì (targeted offers)

ML Algorithms : Decision Trees

What business problem it solves

  • Classification or regression with human-friendly ‚Äúif-then‚Äù logic

Intuition (no math)

  • Splits data into branches like rules: ‚ÄúIf income is high and missed payments are low ‚Üí low risk.‚Äù
  • Very useful for explaining decisions to non-technical stakeholders.

Use when

  • Interpretability matters
  • Policy-style decisioning needed
  • Non-linear relationships exist

Avoid when

  • Need stability and maximum accuracy (single trees may overfit)
  • Data is noisy with many edge cases

Applications + KPI impact

  • Eligibility/underwriting: Speed ‚Üë, Risk ‚Üì
  • Customer service routing: Cost ‚Üì, Speed ‚Üë

ML Algorithms: K-Means Clustering

What business problem it solves

  • Creates customer/product/store segments when you don‚Äôt have labels

Intuition (no math)

  • Groups similar items together based on ‚Äúcloseness‚Äù in behavior (spend, frequency, basket mix).
  • Useful for strategy and marketing differentiation.

Use when / Avoid when

Use when

  • Need segmentation for targeting/positioning
  • No outcome labels exist
  • You want a simple, teachable baseline

Avoid when

  • Segments are highly irregular or overlapping
  • You cannot justify a meaningful number of segments

Applications + KPI impact

  • Customer segmentation: Revenue ‚Üë via personalization, Cost ‚Üì via efficient campaigns
  • Store clustering: improves assortment and replenishment ‚Üí Cost ‚Üì, Speed ‚Üë

ML Algorithms : Neural Networks (Conceptual) + Wrap-Up

What business problem it solves

  • Complex patterns‚Äîespecially for images, text, speech, and large-scale signals

Intuition (no math)

  • Layered learning: early layers detect simple patterns, later layers detect complex features.
  • This family includes many deep learning models used in computer vision and language tasks.

Use when / Avoid when

Use when

  • Unstructured data is central (face unlock, OCR)
  • Large datasets exist
  • Automation of perception is required

Avoid when

  • Small data
  • Strict explainability requirement
  • Tabular problem solvable with simpler models
