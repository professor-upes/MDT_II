New Technology: Who Wins — Incumbents or New Players?

🏛️ The Core Question

When a disruptive technology arrives, does it benefit established firms (incumbents) or hand the advantage to new entrants? The answer depends on whether the technology complements or disrupts existing capabilities.

✅ When Incumbents Win

  • Technology enhances existing assets (brand, data, distribution)
  • High switching costs protect the customer base
  • Incumbents have capital to absorb & integrate the new tech
  • Example: Banks adding mobile payments on top of existing infrastructure

🚀 When New Players Win

  • Technology renders old assets obsolete
  • Incumbents are locked into legacy systems & business models
  • New entrants start native to the new paradigm
  • Example: Netflix vs. Blockbuster; Uber vs. taxi companies

What is Cloud Computing & Why Pay for It?

☁️ Cloud Computing — The Core Idea

Cloud computing has transformed how organizations build, deploy, and scale technology. Instead of owning physical infrastructure, users access computing resources over the internet, on demand.

🚫 No physical servers 📈 On-demand scalability 💸 Pay-as-you-go 🔁 OpEx, not CapEx

💼 Business Reasons to Pay for Cloud Computing

💰 Cost Efficiency

  • Eliminate upfront hardware costs
  • Pay only for what you use
  • Reduce IT maintenance staff costs
  • Shift CapEx → OpEx (predictable billing)

⚡ Speed & Agility

  • Launch new products in days, not months
  • Scale up instantly during peak demand
  • Continuous delivery and daily updates
  • Experiment cheaply and quickly

🌍 Flexibility & Reach

  • Access from anywhere on any device
  • Global deployment in minutes
  • Automatic backups & disaster recovery
  • Latest tech without upgrade cycles

🙋 Who Pays for Cloud Computing?

Payer Type Who They Are What They Use It For
Enterprises Large corporations (e.g., Netflix, Zomato) Run apps, store data, scale globally
Startups New tech companies Launch fast without buying servers
Governments Public sector departments Citizen services, data management
Developers & Students Individuals building projects Host apps, run ML models, experiment
SMEs Small and medium businesses SaaS tools, email, CRM, collaboration

🔑 Key Takeaway: Organizations pay for cloud computing because it converts expensive, slow, fixed infrastructure into a flexible utility — like electricity. You only pay for what you use, and you never worry about the power plant.

On-Premise vs. Cloud: The Car Ownership Analogy

🚗 The Analogy — Own a Car vs. Use a Taxi/Uber

🚘 On-Premise Infrastructure = Owning a Car

As the owner, you pay for it, house it in your personal garage, and maintain it over its life. You can take the car anywhere — but it also sits idle and depreciating when not in use.

Similarly, on-premise infrastructure tends to be underutilized when companies purchase entire servers just to handle small increases in demand. As technology advances, the private owner may become dependent on less professional technicians to maintain an old system while competitors drive the latest.

🏢 On-Premise (Own the Car)

  • Buy & own physical servers
  • Pay full cost upfront (CapEx)
  • Manage maintenance, upgrades, security
  • Scales poorly — buy more hardware for peak loads
  • Hardware depreciates; becomes outdated
  • Best for: Highly regulated industries (some banks, defense)

☁️ Cloud (Use Uber/Taxi)

  • Rent computing power on demand
  • Pay per use (OpEx)
  • Provider manages all maintenance & upgrades
  • Scale up/down instantly with demand
  • Always on latest infrastructure
  • Best for: Most modern businesses & startups

📊 Real-World Comparison

Dimension Legacy IT (On-Premise) Cloud-Native (Transformed)
Spending Model Capital Expenditure CapEx Operational Expenditure OpEx
Delivery Speed 6–12 month project cycles Continuous delivery (daily updates)
Scaling Method Vertical — buy a bigger box Horizontal — add 1,000 small boxes
IT Role Gatekeeper / Cost Center Strategic Partner / Value Enabler

🔑 Key Takeaway: Cloud eliminates the "idle car in the garage" problem. You pay for compute like electricity — only when and as much as you need. This transforms IT from a cost center into a competitive weapon.

Cloud Service Models: IaaS, PaaS & SaaS

Think of it as a spectrum — how much does the cloud provider manage vs. you?

🏗️ IaaS — Infrastructure as a Service

INFRASTRUCTURE AS A SERVICE

What is it?

IaaS provides access to virtualized IT tools — virtual computers, storage, and networks — over the internet. You don't buy or manage physical hardware; you pay only for what you use.

  • Flexibility & Control: Full control over OS and applications
  • Cost Savings: No physical infrastructure investment
  • Scalability: Scale hardware up or down per demand
📦 REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE

Netflix + AWS

Netflix uses AWS (IaaS) to rent thousands of virtual servers to store and stream video globally. They don't own physical hard drives, but retain total control over the operating system and how the streaming software is configured.

Responsibility split:

✅ You: OS, apps, data 🔴 Provider: Hardware, networking, virtualisation

💻 SaaS — Software as a Service

SOFTWARE AS A SERVICE

What is it?

SaaS delivers fully managed software over the internet. You log in and use it — no servers, no code, no updates to manage. The provider handles everything beneath the surface.

  • Zero maintenance: Provider patches, upgrades, secures
  • Mobility: Access from any device, anywhere
  • Focus: Teams concentrate on work, not IT
📧 EXAMPLE: GMAIL

Enterprise Email — The SaaS Way

You need enterprise email but don't want to build a mail server. You log into Gmail. Google manages the servers, security patches, and storage infrastructure.

Responsibility split:

✅ You: User settings & data 🔴 Google: Hardware, software, security, infrastructure

🔑 Key Takeaway: IaaS gives you the land and bricks — you build the house. SaaS gives you a furnished apartment — you just move in. The right model depends on how much control vs. convenience your business needs.
