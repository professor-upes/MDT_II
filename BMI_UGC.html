Business Model Innovation: From Product to Platform

What is BMI?

  • BMI emerged in the 1990s-2000s to explain firm success beyond superior products or cost leadership
  • Core focus: How value is created, delivered, and captured

Web 2.0 and UGC: Platformization

Web 2.0 (2003+) became interactive and social, creating new value logics:

Traditional BM

  • Value by firm alone
  • Products sold to customers
  • IP owned by company
  • Revenue = sales

Web 2.0 Platform

  • Value co-created by users
  • Platforms connect users
  • UGC = core asset
  • Revenue = data, ads

Key Takeaway: The shift from "What we make" to "What users co-create" fundamentally changed value capture.

Supply-Side Scale vs Demand Aggregation

Two fundamental approaches to Business Model Innovation in the digital economy:

Concept Supply-Side Scale Demand Aggregation
Focus Reducing cost per unit by scaling production Increasing platform value by concentrating and coordinating user demand
Economic Logic Efficiency Network effects
Strategic Question "How can we produce and deliver more at lower cost?" "How can we attract, organize, and retain more users to generate value together?"

Supply-Side Scale

  • Approach: Vertical integration and automation
  • Driver: Economies of scale
  • Example: Dell (direct manufacturing)
  • Value: Lower costs = lower prices
  • Challenge: High fixed costs, capital intensive

Demand Aggregation

  • Approach: Platform orchestration
  • Driver: Network effects
  • Example: Uber (connects riders + drivers)
  • Value: More users = more value
  • Advantage: Asset-light, scalable

Key Insight: Supply-side scale dominated industrial/Web 1.0 era. Demand aggregation powers Web 2.0 platforms and drives Business Model Innovation in the digital age.
